# Toward Transparent Causal Representation Learning

*How can we trust a learned causal abstraction if we cannot explain what it corresponds to in the original feature space?*

This question motivated one of the research directions I care about most.

In many modern machine learning settings, especially with high-dimensional temporal data, we do not want to reason directly at the level of raw variables. The observed space is often too granular, too noisy, and too hard to interpret causally. This is exactly why **causal representation learning (CRL)** is attractive: instead of working with thousands of features, we try to learn a smaller latent space whose coordinates correspond to meaningful causal factors.

But there is a problem.

Even when a latent causal model is mathematically elegant, it can still feel opaque in practice. A learned latent factor may improve downstream predictions or counterfactual estimation, yet we may still be unable to answer a simple question:

> **Which observed variables does this factor actually correspond to?**

For images or videos, researchers sometimes rely on visual inspection. For tabular longitudinal data, that strategy is far less helpful. If the input is a large evolving table of transactions, sensor values, or clinical variables, a latent dimension is not visually self-explanatory.

This project was my attempt to make CRL more transparent.

Rather than treating the latent space as a black box, I wanted to recover a meaningful bridge between **latent causal factors** and **structured groups of observed features**.

---

## The problem

A standard CRL setup starts from observed data $X$ and assumes that it is generated from latent variables $Z$ through a decoder or mixing function

$$
X = f(Z, \xi),
$$

where $\xi$ is noise.

The ideal goal is that the latent coordinates $Z_1, \dots, Z_{d_z}$ behave like causal variables: they should admit an underlying causal graph or structural causal model.

That idea is compelling for several reasons:

- the observed space may be extremely high-dimensional,
- causal reasoning is often easier at a more abstract level,
- and learning causal factors is a structured form of disentanglement.

But once we have learned such a latent space, we face a practical interpretability gap.

Suppose a model tells us that a latent factor changed over time and that this change matters causally for the future. What does that actually mean in the original data space?

In simple toy settings, people sometimes enforce strong assumptions such as:

- **single-parent decoding**, where each observed variable depends on only one latent factor, or
- **pure children / anchor features**, where each latent factor has some observed variables that depend only on it.

These assumptions can make interpretation easier, but they are often too restrictive for realistic data. Real features are messy. Some are clean indicators of a single latent cause; others are more entangled.

So the core question of this project became:

> **Can we recover transparent feature groupings from a learned causal decoder without forcing an overly simplistic hard masking structure from the start?**

---

## The central idea

The key idea of the project is geometric.

Instead of inspecting only the values produced by the decoder, I look at its **Jacobian** with respect to the latent variables.

If the decoder is

$$
f(z) = \big(f_1(z), f_2(z), \dots, f_{d_x}(z)\big),
$$

then each observed feature $x_j$ is associated with a gradient vector

$$
\nabla_z f_j(z).
$$

That gradient tells us how the $j$-th observed variable locally reacts to changes in the latent space.

This viewpoint is extremely useful.

If two observed variables depend on the same latent cause, then their gradients should exhibit shared structure. In the simplest case, they may lie on the same one-dimensional subspace, meaning they are locally driven by the same latent direction. More generally, groups of variables generated by the same latent mechanism should induce **clusters of gradients living in common low-dimensional subspaces**.

This leads to the main intuition:

> **Observed variables can be grouped by clustering the geometry of their decoder gradients.**

That is the bridge between abstract latent factors and semantically interpretable subsets of observed variables.

---

## Why Jacobians matter for transparency

I like this perspective because it is more structural than simply looking at correlations between observed variables.

Two variables can be correlated for many accidental reasons. But the Jacobian asks a sharper question:

> *How does each observed coordinate respond when I move in latent space?*

That is much closer to the generative mechanism.

Under strong assumptions such as pure children, the argument becomes especially transparent. If two features are both driven only by the same latent factor, then their gradients must be collinear, and the span of those gradients has rank one. In that case, gradient geometry directly reflects shared latent parentage.

What I found interesting is that this geometric idea also suggests a path beyond those exact assumptions. Even when the structure is more flexible, the Jacobian may still contain enough low-rank organization to reveal meaningful feature groups.

So instead of imposing a brittle hand-crafted mask over the decoder, I regularize the model in a way that encourages the Jacobian itself to become **sparse, self-expressive, and clusterable**.

---

## The modeling assumptions

The project is built around a hybrid structural picture.

I considered longitudinal high-dimensional data, especially settings that are:

- temporal,
- tabular,
- sparse,
- and difficult to inspect visually.

At each time step $t$, the observed variables $x_t$ are generated from latent variables $z_t$ through a decoder, while the latent variables themselves evolve through a temporal causal process.

On the decoder side, I work with a blend of two assumptions:

### 1. Single-parent decoding

Each observed variable may depend on only one latent factor.

This is a very interpretable regime because feature groups become the sets of variables that share the same parent.

### 2. Pure children / anchor features

For each latent factor, there exist at least two observed variables that depend only on that factor.

This is weaker in spirit than requiring all variables to be single-parent. It allows some features to play the role of clean anchors, while others can remain less clean.

### My structural choice

Instead of choosing one assumption and enforcing it everywhere, I combine them:

> each latent factor may either act as a single-parent decoder for some variables, or have at least two pure-child anchor variables.

I find this compromise appealing because it is closer to how real data often behave. Some features are direct signatures of a hidden mechanism; others are noisier or more entangled.

---

## From gradient geometry to subspace clustering

Once the Jacobian enters the picture, a natural mathematical tool appears: **subspace clustering**.

The idea of subspace clustering is simple. We assume that data points in a high-dimensional space do not fill that space arbitrarily, but instead lie near a union of lower-dimensional subspaces. The goal is then to:

1. partition the points by subspace,
2. and recover the structure of those subspaces.

Here, the “data points” are not raw observations. They are the decoder gradients

$$
\nabla_z f_1, \dots, \nabla_z f_{d_x}.
$$

If feature groups share latent parents, then these gradients should organize into a union of low-dimensional subspaces.

That immediately suggests a route to transparency:

- treat the Jacobian columns as a dictionary of geometric objects,
- enforce a sparse self-expressiveness relation among them,
- then cluster the resulting affinity structure.

---

## The regularization I introduced

The self-expressiveness assumption says that each gradient can be reconstructed as a sparse linear combination of the others:

$$
\nabla_z f_j = \mathrm{Jac}(f) \, C_{:,j}, \qquad C_{jj}=0.
$$

In matrix form,

$$
\mathrm{Jac}(f) = \mathrm{Jac}(f) C,
$$

with the diagonal of $C$ constrained to zero to avoid trivial identity solutions.

The coefficient matrix $C$ is supposed to be sparse. Ideally, if a gradient belongs to some subspace $S_\ell$, then it should be reconstructed only from other gradients in the same subspace. In that case, the sparsity pattern of $C$ becomes a map of shared latent structure.

To encourage that behavior during training, I augment the loss of an off-the-shelf CRL model with the penalty

$$
\|C\|_1 + \lambda \|\mathrm{Jac}(f) - \mathrm{Jac}(f) C\|_F^2.
$$

This does two things at once:

- the $\ell_1$ term encourages a sparse coefficient matrix,
- the reconstruction term encourages the Jacobian columns to be self-expressive.

What I like about this formulation is that it is modular.

It is not tied to a single CRL architecture. In principle, any causal representation learning model with a differentiable decoder can be regularized in this way.

So conceptually, the method is not “yet another CRL model.” It is more like a **transparency layer** that can be placed on top of existing CRL systems.

---

## The computational challenge

There is one obvious difficulty.

If the observed dimension $d_x$ is large, then the coefficient matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{d_x \times d_x}$ is large too, and computing Jacobian penalties naively can become expensive.

To make the method practical, I derived a batchwise approximation of the Jacobian penalty using a **Hutchinson trace estimator**.

The high-level trick is to rewrite the Frobenius norm term through a stochastic trace identity, so that the regularization can be approximated from random Gaussian probes instead of explicitly forming and manipulating the full Jacobian penalty in the most expensive way.

This matters because otherwise the method would remain attractive only on paper.

One of the themes I care about in research is turning a mathematically nice idea into something that can actually be trained within the constraints of realistic experimental pipelines.

---

## How feature groups are recovered after training

Once the CRL model has been trained with the Jacobian self-expressiveness regularization, the next step is post-processing.

The coefficient matrix $C$ is turned into a symmetric affinity matrix,

$$
\Omega = \frac{1}{2}\big(|C| + |C|^\top\big),
$$

which defines a graph over observed variables.

- each node corresponds to one observed feature,
- edge weights reflect how strongly two gradients are related through sparse self-expression,
- and clusters are obtained using **spectral clustering**.

The final output is therefore not just a latent representation, but also a **partition of observed variables into groups that share latent geometric structure**.

This is the key transparency payoff of the method.

Instead of saying only “the model learned latent factors,” we can also say:

> “these subsets of observed variables appear to be organized by the same latent mechanism.”

For real tabular data, that is much closer to the kind of explanation practitioners can inspect, question, and communicate.

---

## What I find genuinely interesting about this work

There are several reasons this project matters to me.

### 1. It treats transparency as a structural problem, not a cosmetic one

A lot of interpretability work operates after the fact: saliency maps, local importance scores, or post-hoc explanations layered on top of an opaque model.

Here the goal is different. I wanted transparency to emerge from the **geometry of the generative mechanism itself**.

That feels more principled.

### 2. It is designed for difficult data modalities

Interpretability in CRL is often discussed with images, where visual inspection is natural. But many industrial, transactional, and scientific datasets are not image-like at all. They are high-dimensional evolving tables.

Those are precisely the cases where transparent latent abstractions are both harder and more valuable.

### 3. It relaxes overly rigid sparse-decoder assumptions

Some prior work obtains interpretability by imposing a hard and often simplistic sparse decoding structure. I wanted a more flexible route: one that still respects structural sparsity, but lets it emerge through gradient organization rather than rigid masking.

### 4. It builds a bridge between CRL and subspace methods

I find it exciting when ideas from different literatures meet naturally. Here, causal representation learning connects to sparse subspace clustering through the Jacobian of the decoder. That connection is not just aesthetic; it gives a concrete computational recipe for transparency.

## Experimental story

I evaluated the approach in two settings.

### Synthetic data

The synthetic benchmark was designed to encode a mix of:

- single-parent decoding,
- pure children structure,
- and nested grouping behavior.

The goal was to test whether the learned feature clusters align with the true latent grouping structure.

On this synthetic setup, the CRL + sparse-subspace-clustering pipeline achieved strong clustering coherence, with high homogeneity, completeness, and V-measure relative to a random clustering baseline.

What matters here is not only that the numbers improve, but that the method is recovering the kind of grouped structure it was designed to reveal.

### Real transactional data from Saint-Gobain

I also applied the method to real weekly transaction data.

Here, each $x_t$ is a high-dimensional count vector describing which products were purchased and how often. Importantly, the data come with meaningful external labels that form a nested semantic hierarchy:

- **sub-categories**,
- **categories**,
- **specialties**.

That gives a practical way to evaluate clustering quality. Since we cannot visually inspect whether feature groups are sensible, we compare the learned clusters to these externally meaningful taxonomies using metrics such as:

- homogeneity,
- completeness,
- and V-measure.

The CRL + SSC method substantially outperformed random clustering across all three label granularities.

I find this experiment particularly important because it shows the method working in exactly the kind of regime that motivated the project: real, high-dimensional, non-visual, semantically structured data.

## What this work is really about

At a deeper level, this project is about a question I keep returning to in my research:

> **How do we make learned abstractions scientifically inspectable?**

A latent representation is useful only up to a point if it cannot be related back to the observable world in a structured way.

In causality, this matters even more. If we want latent factors to support explanation, intervention reasoning, or scientific understanding, then it is not enough that they improve a benchmark. We need some principled way to connect them back to groups of real variables.

This project is my attempt to build such a connection.

---

## Limitations and open questions

I want to be careful not to oversell the work.

There are still important theoretical questions to resolve.

### Identifiability of the latent process

If two CRL models induce the same observational distribution and both satisfy self-expressiveness structure, under what additional assumptions do they recover the same latent process up to the usual trivial transformations such as permutation and translation?

That question is fundamental if we want transparency claims to be more than heuristic.

### Subspace preservation of the coefficient matrix

Sparse subspace clustering methods often rely on conditions ensuring that the learned coefficients are **subspace-preserving**, meaning they connect each point only to points from the same subspace.

In this project, an important open issue is to characterize when the Jacobian coefficient matrix $C$ has that property in the CRL setting.

### Dependence on local decoder geometry

The method uses local gradient information, so it is sensitive to how well the decoder geometry reflects the true generative structure. In highly nonlinear settings, the relationship between local Jacobian structure and global semantic grouping can become subtle.

I see these limitations not as weaknesses to hide, but as a research agenda. In fact, some of the most interesting next steps lie exactly here: strengthening the theoretical guarantees behind the geometric picture.

## What I personally contributed and learned

This project reflects several themes that are central to how I think about machine learning research.

First, I care a lot about **interpretable structure in latent-variable models**. I am usually not satisfied with a representation being merely useful; I want to understand what kind of object it is and what kind of scientific claim it supports.

Second, I am drawn to ideas that connect different mathematical viewpoints. Here the key bridge was between:

- causal representation learning,
- sparse generative structure,
- differential geometry through Jacobians,
- and graph-based clustering through sparse self-expression.

Third, I like research questions that arise from practical discomfort. The discomfort here was simple: when CRL is applied to complex tabular temporal data, it is often too hard to explain what the learned latent factors mean. The method grew from taking that discomfort seriously rather than accepting opacity as inevitable.

---

## Takeaway

This project is my attempt to make causal representation learning more transparent in settings where interpretation is genuinely difficult.

The main message is:

> **A latent causal representation becomes much more useful when we can map it back to meaningful groups of observed variables, and decoder Jacobians provide a natural geometric route for doing that.**

I see this work as part of a broader research direction: building representation learning methods whose abstractions are not only causally useful, but also structurally interpretable in the original observational space.
