# Designing a Synthetic Causal World for Forecasting Foundation Models

Most tabular foundation models are pretrained on synthetic supervised tasks. That is a powerful idea: if the synthetic task distribution is broad enough, the model can internalize useful statistical structure before seeing any real benchmark data. But the moment the downstream problem becomes **forecasting**, the question becomes much harder.

A forecasting problem is not just another table. It has chronology, temporal dependence, missingness, drift, regime changes, and strict leakage constraints. So the research challenge I took on was the following:

> **Can we design a synthetic prior for pretraining a tabular foundation model that actually reflects the structure of forecasting tasks?**

My answer was to build a **dynamic structural causal model (DynSCM)** for multivariate time series, then convert each synthetic trajectory into a supervised forecasting table under a strict rolling-origin protocol. The final empirical outcome was mixed, and in parts negative, but the project taught me something deeper than a benchmark win: for forecasting foundation models, the synthetic generator is not a detail. It is the real object of study.

---

## 1. The problem, stated mathematically

Let

$$
X_{1:T} := (X_t)_{t=1}^T, \qquad X_t = (X_t^1,\dots,X_t^p),
$$

where $p \ge 2$ is the number of observed variables and $T$ is the time horizon. The goal is not merely to simulate a time series, but to simulate a family of **forecasting tasks** that can be fed to a tabular learner.

The first design choice was therefore to represent the temporal system as a **time-unrolled causal graph**. The node set is

$$
\mathcal V := \{(j,t) : j \in [p],\; t \in [T]\}.
$$

Edges can be of two kinds:

$$
(k,t-\ell) \to (j,t), \qquad \ell \in \{1,\dots,L\},
$$

for lagged dependencies, and optionally

$$
(k,t) \to (j,t)
$$

for contemporaneous dependencies inside a time slice, provided acyclicity is preserved within that slice.

This yields a **dynamic structural causal model** of the form

$$
\mathcal M = \bigl(\{X^j\}_{j=1}^p, \{U_t^j\}_{t \le T, j \le p}, \{G_t\}_{t \le T}, \{f_{j,t}\}_{t \le T, j \le p}\bigr),
$$

with structural assignments

$$
X_t^j = f_{j,t}\Bigl(\{X_s^k : (k,s) \in \mathrm{Pa}_t(j)\}\Bigr) \oplus U_t^j.
$$

This was the conceptual heart of the project. Instead of generating arbitrary tables, I generated **synthetic dynamic systems** and only then derived forecasting rows from them.

---

## 2. Why the causal formulation mattered

I did not use a causal formalism for decoration. It solved two real modeling problems.

First, it gave me a clean way to encode **temporal acyclicity**. Lagged edges automatically point forward in time, so cycles are excluded by construction. That makes the simulator easier to reason about and easier to stabilize.

Second, it gave me a language for **nonstationarity**. The report explicitly allows an optional latent regime process

$$
S_t \in \{1,\dots,K\},
$$

so the active graph or mechanism can change over time. Rather than saying vaguely that ŌĆ£the distribution drifts,ŌĆØ I can say that the data are generated by a sequence of mechanisms whose active regime may evolve. This is much closer to how real forecasting problems behave. The report also emphasizes regime-specific dynamics, drift, structured missingness, and learnability guards as part of the synthetic design. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file2Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file4Ņłü

## 3. From a trajectory to a forecasting table

A tabular foundation model expects a supervised dataset

$$
\mathcal D = \{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^N.
$$

A forecasting problem starts from a time series. So the first nontrivial step was to define a reduction from one to the other without violating chronology.

I choose a target coordinate $j_y \in [p]$ and define the scalar target series

$$
y_t := X_t^{j_y}.
$$

Then, for an origin--horizon pair $(t,h)$ with $h \in H \subset \mathbb N_{>0}$, the supervised label is

$$
Y^{(t,h)} := y_{t+h} = X_{t+h}^{j_y}.
$$

Each supervised row is therefore indexed by an origin and a horizon:

$$
(x_i,y_i) = \bigl(\phi_{t_i,h_i}(X_{1:t_i}),\; X_{t_i+h_i}^{j_y}\bigr).
$$

The crucial point is that the feature map $\phi_{t,h}$ only sees the past. The implementation enforces the hard constraints

$$
t_i \ge L_{\mathrm{req}}, \qquad t_i + h_i < T, \qquad \max(t_{\mathrm{train}}) < \min(t_{\mathrm{test}}),
$$

which guarantee, respectively:

1. sufficient lookback for all lag-based features,
2. labels strictly in the future,
3. train rows strictly earlier than test rows.

These chronology constraints are central in the reportŌĆÖs benchmark protocol, both for the synthetic tasks and for the real-data evaluation tables. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file5Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file11Ņłü

This is one place where I think the project shows technical maturity: a lot of forecasting work talks about ŌĆ£no leakage,ŌĆØ but here the constraint is explicit and built into the episode construction itself.

## 4. Feature engineering as part of the modeling contract

The row features were not just raw lags. Each row combined several blocks:

- explicit lag values,
- masked kernel summaries over the recent past,
- deterministic channels such as normalized time and horizon,
- optional seasonal encodings,
- and mask channels to expose missingness.

Formally, one of the summary features is of the form

$$
\frac{\sum_{\ell=1}^{L_{\max}} w_{m,\ell} \, z_{t_i-\ell} \, o_{t_i-\ell}}
{\sum_{\ell=1}^{L_{\max}} w_{m,\ell} \, o_{t_i-\ell} + \varepsilon},
$$

where $o_t = \mathbf 1\{z_t \text{ is observed}\}$ is the observation mask. This gives a masked multi-scale summary of the recent history. The report uses this design to expose both short-memory and long-memory behavior while preserving strict train-only preprocessing. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file5Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file10Ņłü

What I like here is that the feature map is doing two jobs simultaneously:

- it is making the problem legible to a table model,
- while still respecting the semantics of forecasting.

That is exactly the kind of interface-design problem that appears in real ML systems work.

## 5. What DynSCM was trying to buy us

The synthetic prior was not intended to be a generic simulator. It was intended to sample tasks from a family of dynamic systems with controlled structure:

- sparse lag dependence,
- optional within-slice effects,
- regime switches,
- drift,
- mixed missingness,
- stable linear cores with bounded nonlinear residuals,
- and target-level learnability filters.

One important theme in the report is **stability by construction**. Synthetic trajectories are easy to make expressive and hard to make trainable. If temporal coefficients are sampled carelessly, trajectories explode, the signal-to-noise ratio becomes meaningless, and pretraining degenerates into noise exposure rather than useful inductive bias.

So a big part of the work was to make ŌĆ£difficultyŌĆØ itself an auditable design object. The report describes budgeted coefficients, stability guards, and even drift parameterizations such as

$$
B_{\ell,t}[k,j] = s_{\ell,k \to j}\, c_j\, w_{\ell,k \to j,t}, \qquad w_{\cdot \to j,t} \in \Delta,
$$

so that drift can evolve while structural budgets remain controlled. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file6Ņłü

That is the difference between a toy simulator and a research-grade prior: the generator is constrained enough to stay meaningful, but flexible enough to express temporal heterogeneity.

## 6. Adapting a tabular in-context learner to forecasting

A second thread of the project was to adapt an in-context tabular learner, NICL, to forecasting **without modifying the model architecture**.

The reduction is simple in principle and subtle in implementation. NICL never sees a raw time series. It only sees the forecasting table,

$$
\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^N,
$$

constructed from origin--horizon pairs under strict chronology.

When a native regression interface is unavailable, the report introduces a quantized surrogate. Continuous targets are discretized into $C$ quantile bins using **train-only** bin edges. The number of classes is chosen by the heuristic

$$
C = \min\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{n}{m_{\min}} \right\rfloor,
\max\bigl(2, \operatorname{round}(\kappa n^{1/3})\bigr)\right),
$$

which comes from the usual bias--variance tradeoff for histogram-like discretization. After inference, predictions are mapped back to the real line through train-bin centers

$$
\mu_k := \frac{1}{|\{i \in \mathrm{train} : c_i = k\}|}
\sum_{i \in \mathrm{train}: c_i = k} y_i,
$$

and, when class probabilities $p_k$ are available, the output is

$$
\hat y = \sum_k p_k \mu_k.
$$

This is not just a workaround. It is a careful way of preserving architecture while keeping the protocol honest: all discretization is train-only, so the evaluation still respects the anti-leakage discipline. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file10Ņłü

## 7. Offline dumps versus live generation

One of the most instructive parts of the project was not a modeling equation but a systems-design lesson.

I initially worked with an **offline curriculum**: generate a fixed synthetic dump, split it deterministically into train and validation, train to convergence, then warm-start the next curriculum stage. This is easy to reproduce, but it has a major weakness: the trainer repeatedly replays a finite synthetic support.

I then moved to a **live profile-driven protocol** where tasks are sampled online from named generator profiles, with explicit train and validation sources, acceptance filters, bounded retries, and profile-controlled evaluation suites. The report contrasts these two protocols directly and explains why the live protocol became the main workflow. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file4Ņłü

In compact form, the shift is this:

$$
\text{offline: } D_k \leadsto \text{replay fixed corpus},
\qquad
\text{live: } \text{sample fresh task } \sim \Pi_{\theta} \text{ at each step}.
$$

This changed the research question. The challenge was no longer only ŌĆ£is the prior rich enough?ŌĆØ It became ŌĆ£is the prior rich enough **and** stable enough to support optimization online?ŌĆØ

That distinction matters a lot in practice.

## 8. The experimental reality

The benchmark setup compared three models under a shared forecasting-table protocol:

1. standard nanoTabPFN,
2. the same architecture pretrained on the DynSCM prior,
3. NICL adapted to forecasting. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file0Ņłü

Real-data performance was evaluated with the standard regression metrics

$$
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i-\hat y_i)^2},
$$

$$
\mathrm{sMAPE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2|y_i-\hat y_i|}{|y_i|+|\hat y_i|},
$$

and

$$
\mathrm{MASE} = 
\frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n |y_i-\hat y_i|}
{\frac{1}{T-m}\sum_{t=m+1}^T |z_t-z_{t-m}|}.
$$

The paired-improvement analysis used

$$
\Delta = \frac{m_{\mathrm{base}} - m_{\mathrm{cand}}}{d(m_{\mathrm{base}})},
$$

with bootstrap confidence intervals over matched rows. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file13Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file14Ņłü

And here is the honest part: the strongest overall benchmark baseline was still **naive last-value forecasting**. On the reported real-data suite, both learned systems remained worse than naive persistence, although NICL often improved over the standard pretrained tabular model. The reportŌĆÖs own aggregated table makes that clear. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file7Ņłü

That is not a glamorous result. But it is a very informative one.

## 9. What the negative result actually taught me

The main lesson was not that synthetic pretraining for forecasting is impossible. The lesson was that there is a major gap between:

- building a synthetic world that looks plausible,
- and building one that induces a **useful pretraining distribution**.

The ablations suggest that once several hard temporal factors co-occur ŌĆö multiple regimes, drift, missingness, longer histories ŌĆö the generator can become variance-dominated before the learner has a chance to exploit the additional realism. This is exactly why the move from offline dumps to live generation mattered: it made these failure modes visible rather than hiding them inside static artifacts. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file4Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn7file14Ņłü

In other words, the problem is not only expressivity. It is **synthetic task geometry**:

- which tasks co-occur in a batch,
- how difficulty is scheduled,
- whether the task remains learnable,
- and whether evaluation really matches the intended downstream contract.

That, to me, is a research-grade insight.

## 10. Why I still consider this a strong portfolio project

A portfolio should not only display polished wins. It should also show how you think when the problem resists easy success.

This project shows several things I would want a research employer or collaborator to notice.

### A. I can formalize a messy modeling problem

The move from forecasting to a tabular pretraining episode was not hand-wavy. It was formalized through explicit constraints, feature maps, and evaluation contracts.

### B. I can design generators, not only train models

The core intellectual contribution was the prior: a dynamic causal generator with regime structure, stability controls, and auditable difficulty knobs.

### C. I can do honest experimental diagnosis

I did not stop at ŌĆ£the model underperformed.ŌĆØ I traced where instability entered: curriculum transitions, co-occurrence of hard factors, and mismatch between synthetic richness and optimization stability.

### D. I understand that protocol is part of the science

The live-vs-offline shift, the train-only preprocessing discipline, and the paired bootstrap analysis are not cosmetic details. They are part of what makes an empirical claim believable.

## 11. What I would do next

If I continue this line of work, I would not start by making the generator even richer. I would start by making it more **operationally disciplined**:

1. tighter generator-side feasibility constraints,
2. stronger control of within-batch heterogeneity,
3. explicit anchoring to persistence-heavy real forecasting statistics,
4. better variance reduction in online task sampling,
5. hybrid priors that mix synthetic causal structure with benchmark-aligned temporal contracts.

The key idea is simple:

$$
\text{better synthetic forecasting priors} \neq \text{more complicated simulators}.
$$

Very often it means:

$$
\text{better controlled task distributions}.
$$

That is the main scientific takeaway I would carry into a second iteration.

---

## Closing reflection

I think this project became stronger once I stopped evaluating it only as a challenge submission and started evaluating it as research.

The benchmark did not end with a clean win. But the project forced me to confront a real question in foundation-model design:

> **What does it actually mean for a synthetic prior to be aligned with temporal prediction?**

My answer was to treat the generator as a dynamic causal system, the forecasting table as a carefully constrained reduction, and the pretraining pipeline as a distribution-design problem rather than a mere training loop.
